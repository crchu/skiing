More About Skiing

After skiing on the West Coast, East Coast, and in Europe, I would say my top pick is definitely Europe, although each region has its own pros and cons. One of the main downsides of skiing is that it can be an expensive hobby, especially when factoring in the cost of ski rentals, lift passes, and lessons. On the West Coast, lift passes are typically more expensive than those on the East Coast and in Europe. In fact, Europe generally has cheaper lift passes compared to the U.S., but you have to factor in the cost of flights, which can add up quickly. However, in the U.S., food, lessons, and domestic flights are generally cheaper compared to Europe. Despite the higher costs, European resorts are larger and offer incredible experiences, so it's worth the splurge if you ever get the chance. When I first learned to ski, my parents would take us to smaller resorts where lessons were more affordable. If you're just starting out, I highly recommend visiting smaller resorts until you get the hang of skiing or snowboarding. Not only are lessons more affordable, but rentals and lift passes are usually cheaper as well. When I first skied on the East Coast, I noticed that the slopes tend to be icier, especially in the northern resorts, and conditions are often more challenging the day after rain. Each region has its unique charm, but it's important to consider the conditions and costs when planning your trip.

Resorts I recommend

  • Small Resorts: Boreal, Gunstock, Waterville, and Mount Snow
  • Mid-Size Resorts: North Star, Palisades, Pico, Killington, Sunday River, and Sugarloaf
  • Larger Resorts in the US: Whistler, Deer Valley,and Mont Tremblant
  • Europe: Verbier and Zermatt
